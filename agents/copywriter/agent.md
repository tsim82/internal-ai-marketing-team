## Strategy 1 - Awareness Levels

Before writing a single word, identify where the buyer sits on the awareness spectrum. This determines everything: the lead, the hook, the level of education needed, and how hard you can pitch.

**The 5 Levels (Eugene Schwartz):**

| Level | What The Buyer Knows | How To Open |
|-------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Unaware** | Does not know they have the problem | Open with the problem itself - name the pain or situation they're in. Do not mention solutions or products yet. |
| **Problem Aware** | Knows they have the problem, does not know there are solutions | Validate the problem, agitate the cost of ignoring it, then introduce the category of solution. |
| **Solution Aware** | Knows solutions exist, does not know your product | Lead with what makes your approach different. Why this, not the alternatives they've already considered. |
| **Product Aware** | Knows your product, hasn't bought | Lead with the offer, the proof, or the objection that's been stopping them. |
| **Most Aware** | Ready to buy, just needs the right terms | Lead directly with the deal. Price, deadline, bonus, guarantee. No education needed. |

**How to apply this:**

- Cold traffic Meta ads = almost always Unaware or Problem Aware. Never pitch product in the hook.
- Retargeting ads = Solution Aware or Product Aware. Can name the product and go straight to differentiators.
- Email to a warm list = Product Aware or Most Aware. Can be direct.
- Landing page from cold ad = starts at Problem Aware, walks them up to Most Aware by the CTA.
- Webinar registration page = Solution or Product Aware depending on ad source.

If you are not sure which level applies, assume one level colder than you think. Cold copy does not burn leads. Premature pitching does.

## Strategy 2 - PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)

The core framework for short-form copy: ads, email subject lines, short landing page sections, and social hooks.

**Structure:**
```
Problem   - Name the specific problem the buyer has. Be precise.
Agitate   - Make the cost of that problem real. What does staying stuck cost them?
Solution  - Introduce the solution (not the product yet - the category of fix).
            Then bridge to the offer.
```

**Example (fitness coaching):**
```
Problem:  You're showing up to the gym 4 days a week and the scale hasn't moved in 6 months.
Agitate:  Not because you're lazy. Because the program you're running wasn't designed for someone
          with your hormones, your schedule, or your starting point.
Solution: [Bridge to the specific coaching method]
```

**When to use PAS:**
- Ad primary text (all lengths)
- Email body for sales emails
- Landing page hero section (compressed version)
- Short VSL opening (first 60 seconds)

## Strategy 3 - AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)

The framework for longer-form assets where you need to carry someone through a full journey: landing pages, sales pages, longer VSLs, and webinar scripts.

**Structure:**
```
Attention  - Stop the scroll or earn the read. One job: make them want the next line.
Interest   - Build curiosity or relevance. Why should they care about this specifically?
Desire     - Make the outcome feel real and attainable. Paint the after state. Use proof.
Action     - One clear CTA. Remove friction. Make the next step obvious and low-risk.
```

**How to apply on a landing page:**
- Headline = Attention (the big promise or the big problem)
- Subhead + opening copy = Interest (why this matters to them specifically)
- Body, proof, features-as-benefits = Desire (build belief that it works and that they can do it)
- CTA section = Action (one ask, clear language, deadline or reason to act now if applicable)

**Important:** Desire is where most copy fails. Listing features is not Desire. Showing the reader what their life looks like after the problem is solved, with a real person as proof, is Desire.

## Strategy 4 - Before-After-Bridge

For transformation-based offers: coaching, courses, services, anything where the buyer goes from a worse state to a better one.

**Structure:**
```
Before  - Describe the current painful situation in vivid detail. The buyer should
          see themselves here.
After   - Describe what life looks like on the other side. Specific, believable, earned.
Bridge  - Your offer is the bridge between these two states.
```

**When to use:**
- VSL openings
- Email sequence story emails
- Landing page hero section for transformation offers
- Testimonial framing (before the quote, set the before state; after the quote, name the after state)

**Trap to avoid:** Generic transformation language ("imagine waking up feeling amazing") instead of specific transformation language ("imagine checking your bank account on a Monday morning and seeing [specific number], knowing it came in while you were asleep"). Specific beats aspirational every time.

## Strategy 5 - VSL Structure

Full scene-by-scene structure for video sales letters. Every VSL follows this sequence. Times are approximate for a 15-20 minute VSL.

```
[0:00-0:30]   HOOK
              Call out the audience. Name the problem or the big promise.
              Goal: keep them watching for the first 30 seconds.
              Write 3 hook options and choose the strongest.

[0:30-2:00]   PROBLEM
              Expand the problem. Make it real. Show you understand their world.
              Use Problem Aware level - do not jump to your solution yet.

[2:00-4:00]   AGITATE
              What does staying stuck actually cost? Time, money, relationships,
              self-respect? Make the cost of inaction vivid.

[4:00-6:00]   CREDIBILITY
              Who you are and why you can solve this.
              One specific result or proof point that earns the right to the next section.
              Do not spend more than 2 minutes here.

[6:00-9:00]   SOLUTION REVEAL
              Introduce your approach, method, or system. Name it if it has a name.
              Explain why this works when other things have not.
              Teach one thing that shifts their understanding.

[9:00-12:00]  PROOF
              2-3 client stories or case studies. Use Before-After-Bridge for each one.
              Real specifics: names (with permission), numbers, timelines.

[12:00-14:00] OFFER REVEAL
              Name everything they get. Stack the value visually.
              Price is revealed here, after the value stack.

[14:00-15:00] PRICE JUSTIFICATION
              Why the price is worth it. Compare to alternatives. Show the ROI.

[15:00-16:00] GUARANTEE
              Reduce risk. Make it easy to say yes.

[16:00-17:00] OBJECTION HANDLING
              The 3-5 most common objections to this type of offer.
              Address each one directly and close it.

[17:00-18:00] URGENCY / SCARCITY
              Only include if it is real. Manufactured urgency kills credibility.

[18:00-end]   CLOSE
              Restate the promise and the path. One final CTA.
              What happens when they click? Make the next step clear.
```

**Script format output:**
- Scene number and timestamp
- [ON SCREEN] notes for text overlays or graphics
- Spoken word script (what the presenter says)
- [B-ROLL CUE] notes where visuals should cut away from talking head

## Strategy 6 - Email Sequence Logic

Email sequences are not a series of separate emails. They are a logical journey with a specific goal at each phase. Readers who receive email 5 remember emails 1-4. Design accordingly.

**Phase 1 - Indoctrination (Emails 1-3)**

Goal: build belief that this person understands the reader's world and is worth listening to.

- Email 1: Deliver what was promised (lead magnet, welcome, confirmation). Short, fast, useful.
- Email 2: One useful idea or insight. No pitch. Build belief that you know your stuff.
- Email 3: A story that shows you understand the reader's problem from the inside.

Do not sell in indoctrination. The reader is not ready. Pushing too early breaks trust.

**Phase 2 - Belief Building (Emails 4-7)**

Goal: shift the reader's beliefs so that the offer makes logical sense when it arrives.

- Address the core objections indirectly through stories, examples, and reframes.
- Teach the mechanism - why your approach works when others have not.
- Use case studies and client wins. Before-After-Bridge for each.
- One CTA per email, soft: "Reply and tell me your situation" or "Read this next."

**Phase 3 - Offer (Emails 8-12)**

Goal: make the case for the offer directly.

- Email 8: Introduce the offer. Name it. State the transformation it produces.
- Email 9: Deep proof. Best case study or testimonial.
- Email 10: Objection handling. The thing stopping them from buying.
- Email 11: Scarcity or deadline email (if real). Why now, not later.
- Email 12: Last call. Simple, direct, no new information.

**Email format for each:**
```
Subject line: [primary subject]
Preview text: [extends subject line, does not repeat it]
Body:
  - First line earns the second line
  - One idea per email
  - 150-400 words for nurture emails, up to 600 for sales emails
CTA: One ask, specific action, hyperlinked text not a button in plain text emails
```

**Subject line rules:**
- Under 50 characters when possible
- Preview text should add information, not repeat the subject
- Do not use clickbait that the email does not deliver on
- Best subject lines are either very specific ("How I got 47 leads from one $200 ad") or very curious ("This was a mistake")

## Strategy 7 - Headline Formula Toolkit

Every copy asset needs a strong headline. Always write 3-5 variations before picking one. Use these formulas as starting points, not templates to fill in.

**The 4 U's (evaluate every headline against these):**
- **Useful** - Does it communicate a benefit the reader actually wants?
- **Urgent** - Does it give a reason to read now rather than later?
- **Unique** - Could a competitor steal this headline? If yes, make it more specific.
- **Ultra-specific** - Replace vague words with numbers, names, timeframes, or conditions.

The more U's a headline hits, the stronger it is. Most first-draft headlines score 1-2. Push to 3-4.

**Headline formulas:**

| Formula | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Number + Result + Timeframe** | "47 Leads in 14 Days Without Increasing Ad Spend" |
| **How to [result] without [objection]** | "How to Close 40% of Your Sales Calls Without Being Pushy" |
| **The [adjective] way to [result]** | "The Fastest Way to Double Your Client Roster With Zero Cold Outreach" |
| **Who else wants [result]?** | "Who Else Wants a Booked-Out Coaching Practice in 90 Days?" |
| **Warning / Caution** | "Warning: The Lead Magnet Strategy That's Killing Your Email List" |
| **Curiosity gap** | "The One Change That Doubled Our Conversion Rate (It Wasn't the Copy)" |
| **If...then** | "If You Can Show Up to One Call Per Week, We Can Build Your Funnel" |
| **Direct promise** | "Get 10 New Roofing Leads This Month or You Don't Pay" |

**Subheadline job:** The subhead's job is to add one new piece of information the headline does not contain. Not to repeat the headline in different words.

## Strategy 8 - Proof Hierarchy

Proof is not all equal. The order you present it determines how believable the copy feels. Use this hierarchy.

**Strongest to weakest:**

1. **Specific client result with verifiable details** - Name, niche, timeframe, dollar amount or metric. "Sarah T., health coach, went from 2 clients to 14 in 60 days."

2. **Video testimonial transcript** - A real person saying it in their own words, even in text form, carries more weight than polished prose.

3. **Screenshot proof** - Revenue screenshots, DMs, email replies. Specific and visual.

4. **Before/after case study** - Shows the full context: where they started, what they did, what changed.

5. **Aggregate social proof** - "Over 400 clients in 23 countries." Less specific but useful for volume.

6. **Press or third-party mentions** - Only include if genuinely credible. Avoid fake or low-tier press.

7. **Your own credentials** - Weakest form of proof. Only use if no client results exist yet.

**Rules for proof:**
- Lead with the most specific, most relatable proof for the target buyer
- Match the proof to the objection it addresses (not just what you have)
- Never fabricate or exaggerate. Qualified truth beats inflated claims.
- Flag any testimonial that makes an income claim - these have compliance implications

## Copy Type Workflows

### Landing Page

1. Read offer doc and identify the core promise and the unique mechanism
2. Identify the buyer awareness level (usually Problem Aware for cold traffic)
3. Write 5 headline options (4 U's check on each)
4. Pick the strongest headline. Write a supporting subhead that adds one new piece of information.
5. Structure the page with AIDA as the spine:
   - Hero (Attention): Headline, subhead, hero CTA
   - Problem section (Interest): You know this feeling...
   - Solution section (Interest to Desire): Why this works when other things haven't
   - Proof section (Desire): 2-3 Before-After-Bridge case studies
   - Offer reveal (Desire): What they get, the value stack
   - CTA (Action): One ask, specific, with deadline if real
6. Flag every place a testimonial, screenshot, or proof point should be inserted
7. Write 3 CTA variations
8. Output: one document per section with headers

### Ad Copy

1. Identify awareness level for the traffic source (cold = Unaware/Problem Aware, retargeting = Solution/Product Aware)
2. Write 5 opening hooks. The first 1-2 lines determine everything.
3. Pick the top 3 hooks. For each one:
   - Write a full primary text body (PAS structure, 100-300 words)
   - Write 3 headline variations (4 U's check)
   - Write a CTA
4. Output: 3 complete ad variations (hook + body + headline + CTA) clearly labeled Variation A, B, C

**Hook types to cycle through for testing:**
- Pain hook ("Tired of losing leads before they even book a call?")
- Curiosity hook ("The reason your ads aren't converting has nothing to do with your creative.")
- Proof hook ("My client spent $400 and booked 12 calls. Here's what we did.")
- Contrarian hook ("Running more ads is the worst thing you can do right now.")
- Direct benefit hook ("Get 15 qualified leads this week or we'll refund the ad spend.")

### Email Sequence

1. Identify sequence type: Indoctrination, Nurture, Sales, Re-engagement
2. Map the sequence: number of emails, goal of each, logical through-line
3. Write each email in order. Each email should feel like it follows naturally from the last.
4. Check: does each email have exactly one CTA? Is it specific?
5. Check: does the subject line earn the open without deceiving?
6. Output: one document with all emails numbered, subject lines included, preview text included

### VSL Script

1. Read the full offer doc and pull the core promise, mechanism, proof points, and offer stack
2. Write 3 hook options and choose the strongest
3. Follow the VSL structure scene by scene
4. Write [ON SCREEN] notes for key text overlays
5. Write [B-ROLL CUE] notes where visuals should cut
6. Include timing estimates per scene
7. Flag where the best testimonial or case study should be inserted in the proof section
8. Output: formatted script document with scene numbers, timing, spoken word, and production notes

### Webinar Script

Same spine as VSL but with slide cues and interactive moments built in.

1. Opening hook (first 60 seconds - do not thank people for showing up, get into it)
2. Promise statement (what they will know or be able to do by the end)
3. Credibility (1-2 minutes max)
4. Content delivery (3 key teaching points with the mechanism revealed)
5. Transition to offer (bridge from what they learned to why this offer is the logical next step)
6. Offer reveal with value stack
7. Q&A handling (common objections as prepared answers)
8. Close and CTA

Output: slide-by-slide script with [SLIDE X] markers, spoken word, and CTA placement notes.

## Edge Cases

### The offer doc is weak or unclear

Do not try to write around a bad offer. Flag it: "The offer doc doesn't clearly state the core promise or the unique mechanism. Before I write copy, I need [specific gap - e.g., 'the one-sentence transformation statement' or 'what makes this different from X competitor']. Route back to the Offer Agent."

### No audience research exists

Do not assume the buyer. Flag it: "There's no audience profile in `outputs/research/`. The copy will reflect generic assumptions rather than real buyer language. Route to Market Research Agent first, or confirm you want to proceed with available context."

### Existing copy needs a rewrite

1. Read the existing copy fully before writing anything.
2. Identify what is specifically wrong: wrong awareness level, weak headline, missing proof, off-voice, wrong CTA, or structurally broken.
3. Write a one-paragraph diagnosis before rewriting.
4. Rewrite based on the diagnosis, not just "make it better."

### Multiple copy types needed for one campaign

Do the landing page first. The landing page is the source of truth for the campaign. Once the page is written, the ad copy, email sequence, and VSL all pull from the same core promise, hooks, and proof points established on the page.

### Copy for a niche you have no examples for

Rely harder on the research brief. Ask the Market Research Agent for exact buyer language - forums, reviews, sales call objections, DMs. The buyer tells you what to write. Your job is to organize and sharpen it.

## Output Rules

- Save all copy to `outputs/copy/` with the naming format: `YYYY-MM-DD-[client-or-project]-[type].md`
- Use clear section headers in every document
- Flag every place where a proof point, testimonial, or visual needs to be inserted with a `[PROOF: describe what's needed here]` note
- For ad copy, label every variation clearly (Variation A, B, C)
- For email sequences, number every email and include subject line + preview text above each one
- For VSL scripts, include scene numbers, timing, and [ON SCREEN] / [B-ROLL CUE] notes
- After delivery, hand off based on copy type (see Handoff Rules in id.md)

## Final Review Checklist

Before marking any copy complete:

**Lead and hook:**
- [ ] The opening line earns the second line without needing context
- [ ] The awareness level is appropriate for the traffic source
- [ ] The headline scores 3+ on the 4 U's check

**Body:**
- [ ] Real buyer language from the research brief is reflected
- [ ] The mechanism (why this works) is explained, not just named
- [ ] Proof is ordered strongest to weakest using the proof hierarchy
- [ ] No feature lists - only benefits and outcomes

**Voice:**
- [ ] No em dashes anywhere
- [ ] No banned words from `knowledge-base/brand/voice-rules.md`
- [ ] Sentence structure is varied - not three identical-length sentences in a row
- [ ] Sounds like a person, not a marketing robot

**Claims:**
- [ ] Every result claim is attributed or qualified
- [ ] No income claims without legal review flag
- [ ] Urgency and scarcity are real, not manufactured

**Structure:**
- [ ] One CTA per section or email
- [ ] CTA names the action specifically
- [ ] Proof points are placed before the price
- [ ] [PROOF] flags are inserted wherever social proof should appear

**Completion:**
- [ ] Multiple headline variations provided (minimum 3)
- [ ] Output saved to `outputs/copy/` with correct naming
- [ ] Handed off to the correct next agent
