# Brand Voice Rules

These rules apply to every agent, every output, and every piece of content produced by the internal team. QA Agent checks against these before anything goes live.

---

## Writing Rules

- Never use em dashes.
- Use contractions. (It is fine to write "you're" and "we'll.")
- Write in plain English.
- Sound conversational and natural.
- Do not sound corporate.
- Do not use AI hype language.
- Do not use rhythmic three-part phrasing.
- Do not use repetitive sentence patterns.
- Do not over-explain unless the task requires it.
- Write like you are explaining the work over coffee.
- Focus on outcomes, not abstract theory.
- Make the output useful for an internal agency team or a real buyer.
- Keep client-facing copy clean, direct, and believable.

## Sentence Structure Rules

- Vary sentence length. Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones.
- Do not use the same sentence structure three times in a row.
- Do not open every sentence with "The" or "This."
- Do not end every paragraph with a rhetorical question.
- Do not stack adjectives. Pick one.

---

## Format Rules

- Use headers to organize longer outputs.
- Use bullet points for lists of 3 or more items.
- Do not use bold for decoration. Use it only for genuinely important terms or labels.
- Do not use ALL CAPS for emphasis in copy intended for reading. Use it sparingly in headlines or buttons only.

---

## Tone Guidelines

**Internal documents (plans, briefs, reports):**
Practical, direct, efficient. Like a sharp colleague who respects your time.

**Client-facing copy (landing pages, emails, ads):**
Warm, credible, conversational. Like a trusted advisor who has seen this problem before and knows how to fix it.

**Social content:**
Natural and human. Like a real person sharing something useful or interesting, not a brand account.

---

## What To Avoid In Every Context

- Overpromising results without qualification
- Vague transformational language ("your life will change forever")
- Fake urgency that is obviously manufactured
- Corporate hedging language that says nothing
- Trendy marketing slang that will age badly

---

## Extended Banned Phrases

These are common in marketing copy and should be avoided for the same reason as the list above - they are generic, vague, or worn out:

- "next level"
- "at the end of the day"
- "in today's world"
- "now more than ever"
- "don't miss out"
- "the secret to"
- "proven system"
- "step-by-step blueprint"
- "limited spots available" (unless it is literally true)
- "join thousands of" (unless you have the number and it is verified)
- "I used to struggle with" (as an opener - it is a cliche)
- "if you're tired of" (as an opener - it is a cliche)
- "imagine a world where"
- "the truth about"
- "what they don't want you to know"
- "are you ready to"
- "it's time to"
- "stop dreaming, start doing"
- "turn your passion into profit"

## Copy-Type Specific Rules

### Landing Pages And Sales Pages

- Lead with the outcome the buyer wants, not with a description of the product
- Put the biggest, most specific result first
- Headlines should be specific enough that a competitor could not steal them
- CTAs name the action: "Book my free strategy call" not "Get started"
- Proof (testimonials, case studies, stats) comes before the price
- Price is always followed by justification for why it is worth it
- Long-form pages need clear section breaks - do not let the reader get lost
- Do not bury the offer. Name it clearly in the first 300 words.

### Email Copy

- Subject lines under 50 characters when possible
- Preview text should add something the subject line does not say
- Email 1 in a sequence delivers what was promised, fast
- Keep the first email short - one clear action
- Do not explain the entire offer in the first email
- Each email should end with exactly one CTA, not three
- Re-engagement emails should acknowledge the gap honestly, not pretend it did not happen

### Ad Copy

- First line of primary text must stop the scroll - treat it like a headline
- Write for the feed, not for a web page. The reader did not come to read an ad.
- State the pain or the desire first. Then the offer.
- Do not explain features in ads. Explain what the buyer's life looks like after.
- Headlines in image ads: 5-8 words maximum, high contrast
- CTA button text should match what the landing page headline promises

### Social Content

- First line is everything. It is the only line most people read before deciding.
- Do not end posts with three call-to-action questions
- Opinions and takes outperform tips in organic social
- Write for the target reader, not for everyone
- Do not post content that could have been written by anyone in any niche
- LinkedIn gets more formal but not corporate
- Instagram and Facebook get more personal and direct

### Internal Documents

- State the conclusion first, then the reasoning
- Use numbered lists when order matters, bullet points when it does not
- No filler openers like "Great question" or "To summarize what we discussed"
- Do not write more than is needed. A plan does not need an introduction.

## Proof Standards

Claims in copy must meet one of these standards before the QA Agent passes them:

**Verified** - You have documented evidence (screenshot, report, client statement) that supports the claim exactly as written.

**Attributed** - The claim comes from a named client with their consent. Write it as a quote or a case study, not as a general promise.

**Qualified** - The claim is plausible and typical but not guaranteed. Add qualification language: "results vary," "based on [X type of client]," "in our experience working with [audience]."

**Conservative** - If in doubt, dial the claim down. A claim that slightly undersells is more credible than one that oversells.

**Flag for review** - If you are not sure whether a claim meets the above standards, do not include it. Flag it for the team to verify before the content goes live.

Claims that do not meet any of these standards get removed, not softened.
