# Local Authority Signals

## Purpose

Audit and improve the local authority signals that help a brand appear in AI responses for location-specific queries. Local authority signals are different from general entity signals - they focus on NAP consistency, local directory presence, local citations, and community relevance. Relevant for service businesses with a local or regional footprint.

## Used By

- GEO Agent (primary)
- Marketing Director (reviews for local clients)

## Trigger

Use for any client with a local or regional service area, when a client is not appearing in local AI queries, or when "near me" or city-specific queries return competitors but not the client.

## Inputs Needed

- Business name (exact legal name and any DBA names) - required
- Full physical address (or service area if no storefront) - required
- Phone number - required
- Business category (primary and secondary) - required
- Geographic target area (city, metro, region) - required
- Existing Google Business Profile - required
- Main competitors in the local market - required

## Process

**Step 1: Audit the Google Business Profile**

The Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most important local authority signal. AI tools reference GBP data heavily for local queries.

Check each element:
- [ ] Business name: exact legal name (no keyword stuffing)
- [ ] Primary category: the most accurate category available
- [ ] Secondary categories: any relevant additional categories
- [ ] Address: matches the website contact page exactly
- [ ] Phone number: matches all other local listings
- [ ] Business hours: current and accurate
- [ ] Website URL: live and correct
- [ ] Description: complete, uses the canonical entity description
- [ ] Photos: at minimum 10 photos (interior, exterior, team, work examples)
- [ ] Q&A section: pre-populated with common questions and accurate answers
- [ ] Reviews: response rate (has the business responded to recent reviews?)
- [ ] Posts: has the business posted in the last 30 days?

**Step 2: Audit NAP consistency**

NAP = Name, Address, Phone. Must be identical across every online presence.

Common NAP inconsistencies that create authority problems:
- "LLC" included in some listings but not others
- Suite number or abbreviation variation (Suite 100 vs Ste 100)
- Phone number format variation (555-123-4567 vs (555) 123-4567)
- Old address on older directories

Check NAP across:
- Google Business Profile
- Website contact page
- Yelp
- Facebook Business
- LinkedIn Company
- Apple Maps
- Bing Places
- Industry-specific directories

Document every discrepancy with the source and what needs to change.

**Step 3: Audit local directory presence**

Compare the brand's directory presence to top local competitors.

| Directory | Competitor 1 | Competitor 2 | Our brand |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Google Business Profile | [Yes / No] | [...] | [...] |
| Yelp | [...] | [...] | [...] |
| Apple Maps | [...] | [...] | [...] |
| Bing Places | [...] | [...] | [...] |
| [Niche-specific directory] | [...] | [...] | [...] |
| Better Business Bureau | [...] | [...] | [...] |
| Chamber of Commerce | [...] | [...] | [...] |

Any directory where a competitor is listed and the brand is not is a gap to close.

**Step 4: Audit local citations and community relevance**

Beyond directories, local authority comes from citations in local community context:
- Local news coverage or features
- Local business association memberships
- Sponsorships of local events
- Featured in local "best of" lists
- Community organization affiliations

These tell AI tools that the business is genuinely part of the local community.

**Step 5: Audit review volume and quality**

AI tools use reviews as authority signals. Check:
- Total review count on Google (and other platforms)
- Average rating
- Recency of reviews (have any been added in the last 90 days?)
- How the brand compares to top local competitors on review volume

If a competitor has 3x the review count, that is an authority gap that affects AI visibility for local queries.
