# Pricing Positioning

## Purpose

Determine how to frame and present the price in marketing materials so that it feels justified, logical, and even cheap relative to alternatives or the cost of the problem. Pricing positioning is not about setting the price (see cfo/pricing-analysis.md) - it is about how the price is introduced, anchored, and justified in copy and sales conversations.

## Used By

- Offer Agent (defines the positioning)
- Copywriter (executes the framing in copy)
- CRM Follow-Up Agent (uses the anchoring in sales conversations)

## Trigger

Use when writing VSL copy, sales page copy, or any content that reveals a price. Also use when a client says "prospects keep saying it's too expensive" or when the current copy does not explain why the price is what it is.

## Inputs Needed

- Offer price - required
- Completed offer buildout and offer stack - required
- Competitor price range - required
- Audience income or budget range - helpful
- Main objections related to price (from objection research) - helpful
- Any LTV or ROI data available - helpful

## Process

**Step 1: Select the anchor**

A price anchor is what the buyer compares the price to. The anchor determines whether the price feels expensive or cheap.

Four anchor types:
1. **Competitor anchor**: "Other agencies charge $5,000-10,000/month for what's included here"
2. **Build-it-yourself anchor**: "If you hired a copywriter, media buyer, and strategist separately, you'd spend $7,000-15,000/month in salaries"
3. **Cost-of-problem anchor**: "Most businesses in this position are leaving $3,000-5,000/month on the table from a broken funnel"
4. **Value stack anchor**: "Everything in this offer totals $[X] in market-rate value"

Pick the anchor that is most honest and most impactful for this audience. Multiple anchors can be layered.

**Step 2: Decide the price reveal sequence**

When does the price appear, and what context has been established before it is shown?

Strong sequence:
1. Establish the problem and its cost (anchor the pain)
2. Introduce the solution and its value (anchor the outcome)
3. Show the stack value total (anchor the comparison)
4. Reveal the price (after the anchor has made the price feel small)
5. Immediately introduce the guarantee (reduce the risk)

Weak sequence: Lead with the price before any of the above are established.

**Step 3: Write the price framing copy**

The price reveal paragraph should do three things:
1. Reference the anchor ("That's a fraction of what it would cost to...")
2. State the price clearly without apology
3. Immediately move to the guarantee

Do not apologize for the price or hedge it ("It might seem like a lot, but..."). Confidence in the price reflects confidence in the result.

**Step 4: Break down the price if it helps**

For high-ticket offers, breaking the price into a daily or weekly equivalent can reduce the perceived weight.

"$3,000/month is $100/day - roughly what most businesses spend on a single mediocre hire, minus the overhead."

Only do this if the comparison is genuine and resonates with the audience. Do not use it as a trick.

**Step 5: Handle the price objection proactively**

The top price objections are usually:
- "I can't afford it right now" (cash flow, not value objection)
- "I don't know if it will work for me" (risk objection, not price objection)
- "I found someone cheaper" (perceived value gap)

Match the framing to the real objection. Trying to reduce the price for a risk objector does not help - they need a guarantee. Trying to explain value to a cash flow objector does not help - they may need payment plans.

## Output Format

```
PRICING POSITIONING - [Client/Offer] - [Date]

OFFER PRICE: $[X]

---

SELECTED ANCHOR(S):

Primary anchor: [Type - Competitor / Build-it-yourself / Cost-of-problem / Value stack]
Anchor statement: "[Copy-ready phrase for the Copywriter]"
Anchor value: $[X] (what it is being compared against)

Secondary anchor (if applicable): [Same format]

---

PRICE REVEAL SEQUENCE:

1. Establish problem cost: [Brief note on what to say about the cost of the problem]
2. Introduce the stack value: [Stack total $[X]]
3. Reveal price: $[X]
4. Immediately transition to guarantee: [Guarantee summary]

---

PRICE FRAMING COPY (draft for Copywriter):

"[Full paragraph - anchor, price reveal, and guarantee transition]"

---

PRICE BREAKDOWN (if applicable):
$[X]/month = $[X]/day
Comparison: "[What $[X]/day is equivalent to in terms the audience understands]"

---

PRIMARY PRICE OBJECTION FOR THIS AUDIENCE:
Objection: [Most common price-related objection for this buyer]
Type: [Cash flow / Risk / Perceived value gap]
Response: [How to address it in copy or on a call - one paragraph]

---

COPY NOTES:
Do not: [Any framings to avoid for this specific audience or offer]
Do: [Specific language or comparisons that resonate for this market]
```

## Quality Check

- [ ] At least one anchor is selected and the anchor value is sourced (not made up)
- [ ] Price reveal sequence is documented
- [ ] Price framing copy does not apologize for or hedge the price
- [ ] Primary price objection is identified and addressed
- [ ] Framing is appropriate to the audience's actual financial position
